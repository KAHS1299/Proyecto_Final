CRISP-ML Methodology

From tourism problem to monitored AI service

The platform is organized around six CRISP-ML phases: business understanding, data engineering, model engineering, evaluation, deployment, and monitoring.

01

Business Understanding & Data

Tourism overcrowding affects mobility, hotel availability, public services, resident quality of life, and visitor satisfaction in heritage towns.

1. Business Objectives and Context

The Core Problem

Tourism in Colombia's heritage towns represents a fundamental economic engine for the regions. However, its extreme variability generates critical operational challenges. During high seasons and specific dates (such as Holy Week or traditional long weekends), flagship destinations like Villa de Leyva, Barichara, or Salento experience an overcapacity phenomenon. This massive and sudden influx causes the collapse of access road infrastructure, immediate saturation of hotel capacity, and a general degradation in the quality of the experience for both visitors and local residents.

🎯 Main Goal of the ML Project

Design, train, and deploy a predictive model based on the Random Forest algorithm capable of intelligently anticipating tourist congestion levels (Classified as Low, Medium, or High). The system will function as an operational intelligence tool to allow actors in the tourism ecosystem to plan preventive strategies.

Expected Impact (Stakeholders)
🚶 Tourists

Informed decision-making to modify travel dates or book in advance, improving their experience.

🏛️ Local Authorities

Early activation of road contingency plans and reinforcement of public cleaning and security services.

🏨 Hotel Sector

Rate optimization (Dynamic Pricing) and preparation of inventory and staff for demand peaks.

2. Key Business Questions

For the Artificial Intelligence model to generate real value, the data architecture and classification algorithm have been specifically designed to answer three levels of analytical questions:

  • 01.Descriptive Level (Diagnosis)

    What are the environmental and calendar factors (such as prevailing weather, presence of national holidays, or school seasons) that show the highest mathematical correlation with saturation peaks in heritage municipalities?

  • 02.Predictive Level (Prognosis)

    Is it possible to predict with a high level of statistical confidence the saturation level (Low, Medium, High) of a specific destination in a given month of the year, based solely on variables that can be known months in advance?

  • 03.Prescriptive Level (Actionability)

    If the system predicts a "High" saturation level for Guatapé or Santa Fe de Antioquia, what business rules or automated recommendations can be issued on the user interface to mitigate the negative impact of massive vehicular flow from the main city?

3. Data Structure Evaluation

To model tourist flow behavior, a custom-engineered dataset was consolidated, comprising records from multiple heritage towns, structured into the following mathematical dimensions (Features):

📍 Spatial and Contextual Variables
  • town (Categorical): Unique identifier of the municipality.
  • lat and lon (Numerical): Exact geographic coordinates. Allow the model (if spatial distances are used) to understand proximity to large cities that inject tourists.
📅 Temporal Variables
  • month (Discrete): Month of the year, crucial for capturing intrinsic seasonalities.
  • season (Ordinal): General season label (Low, Medium, High).
  • holiday (Binary): Critical indicator of long weekends or national rest days.
🌤️ Local Dynamics Variables
  • weather (Categorical): Expected prevailing weather condition.
  • mobility (Ordinal): Historical level of pressure on land access roads.
  • events (Discrete): Density or magnitude of cultural events that act as visitor magnets outside the season.
🎯 Target Variable (Target Y)
  • saturation_level (Categorical): The final label that the Random Forest Classifier model will learn to predict based on the cross-behavior of all the independent variables (X Matrix) mentioned above.

4. Technical Viability and Risk Management

The mathematical viability of the project is high because the selected algorithm (Random Forest) is robust at handling complex combinations of categorical data (like weather) and numerical data (like events), creating multiple decision trees that avoid traditional Overfitting. However, the architecture imposes strict control over the data flow:

The Critical Risk: Data Leakage

The original dataset includes post-trip historical variables such as occupancy (Real hotel occupancy percentage) and estimated_tourists (Final volume calculation). The risk of Data Leakage occurs if a data scientist includes these variables in the predictive model's training.

The Architectural Measure:

It was established in the Feature Engineering pipeline (Phase 2) that these variables are excluded and purged from the training matrix X. This ensures that the algorithm does not "cheat" by memorizing results, forcing the neural network/ensemble to infer congestion based solely on anticipatable factors (month, holidays, weather), thus simulating a 100% real prediction scenario for a production application.
